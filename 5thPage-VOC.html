VOCs and Plants

Watch Out Plants!

Not only does ozone hurt humans, but it also harms plants. The United States Department of Agriculture even stated that “ground-level ozone causes more damage to plants than all other air pollutants combined”. Ozone enters plants through their stomata - pores that allow gas exchange to occur. Once the ozone is inside the plant, it dissolves in water and causes a series of problems for the plant. Plants grow slower since photosynthesis is deterred, and energy production in the cell’s mitochondria is hindered.

Symptoms

Several plants clearly display symptoms of overexposure to ozone. Common indications of ozone include: chlorosis - leaves lose chlorophyll and become yellow, pale, or white, necrosis - leaves start to turn black or brown because the plant tissue is dying, purpling - leaves start to turn purple, or stippling - small, irregular, dark spots form on the leaves.

Experiment

There are several plants that can be used as bioindicators of ozone. I was curious to see how humans are killing plants by using toxic cleaning detergents around their house. Febreze is known to have over 70 air pollutants, so I tested how badly plants were affected by Febreze as compared to a homemade nontoxic cleaner.

Procedure

  1. Transfer the spinach plants to the cups
  2. Label the cups “Control 1”, “Control 2”, “VOC 1”, “VOC 2”, “Nontoxic 1”, and “Nontoxic 2”
